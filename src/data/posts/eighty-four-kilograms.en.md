## A new number worth knowing

The European Environment Agency's 2025 report puts Hungarian household food waste at about 84 kilograms per person, per year. That's a few kilos under the EU average — and a few kilos above what we'd been quoting on our own website. We've been carrying the older 65 kg figure for months. Time to update it.

Eighty-four kilograms per person, per year. In a four-person household, that's roughly 336 kilograms — the weight of a small piano — finding its way from a Tesco bag, through a fridge, to a bin.

Most of it could have stayed food.

## Where the kilograms actually go

The Hungarian agri-food research that's been quietly building over the last three years has a useful breakdown. Bread and bakery items are the largest single category — partly because Hungarians eat a lot of bread and partly because bread goes stale faster than anyone plans for. Then vegetables, mostly the ones bought "for the week" and forgotten in the bottom drawer. Then leftovers from cooked dishes that nobody finished and that, by day four, nobody quite trusted.

None of that is moral failure. It's the math of optimistic shopping plus the physics of a small fridge.

## A quietly important change in 2024

You may not have noticed it, but as of early 2024 Hungary began the staged rollout of separate biowaste collection — the brown bins, the green caddies, the kitchen scrap bags. The point isn't that throwing food into a brown bin is somehow okay. It isn't. The point is that a household which has to physically separate its food waste suddenly *knows*, in a way it didn't before, exactly how much of its weekly shop ended up uneaten.

When you can see the bin filling up with cucumbers you bought in good faith on Sunday, the next Sunday's shopping list gets shorter on its own.

That's a lever. We want to pull it.

## How Kalmio is using this

The grooming ritual already exists for this reason. Before each new plan, we walk you through the fridge, you tap *keep / throw out / less than this*, and the items nearest expiry get pinned to the upcoming week so the planner pulls them in first. That part shipped.

What's new is what we're now logging on top of it.

Every "throw out" decision becomes a small data point: ingredient, quantity, days from purchase, days from expiry. After a few weeks of plans, the system has a private picture — yours, never shared, never aggregated for anyone else — of *which items consistently leave your fridge uneaten*. Two months in, the planner starts quietly reducing those defaults. Eight days of cucumber become four. The Sunday yogurt purchase shrinks from two big tubs to one. Nobody told you to buy less. The plan just stopped overbuying.

The goal is small: every Hungarian Kalmio user should see their bin get lighter by the end of the year. Not because they tried harder. Because the plan got smarter about what their household actually eats.

## What we're not claiming

We are not solving food waste. That problem is bigger than an app and tangles with retailers, packaging law, household income, and a dozen other things we don't control. What we *are* claiming is that an honest, fridge-aware planner can, for one household, reliably trim a meaningful slice off the 84-kilogram number. We'll publish what we see — in aggregate, with consent — once we have enough months of data to say something serious.

Eighty-four kilograms is a lot of food to throw away. Encouragingly, it's also a number we can move.
