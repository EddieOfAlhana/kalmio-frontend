## The Finnish village that changed a country

In the 1960s, the men of North Karelia, a rural province in eastern Finland, were dying of heart disease at the highest rate in the world. Heavy smoking, butter on everything, lard, vodka, salt-cured meat. Decade after decade, men in their forties were dropping dead, and the country was talking about it.

Then a young epidemiologist named Pekka Puska, in 1972, started something stubborn. Not a medical intervention. A *cultural* one. He worked with farmers, dairies, mothers' clubs, schools, supermarkets, churches. He helped reformulate sausages to use less salt. He convinced dairy cooperatives to make low-fat options affordable. He got grandmothers to teach grandchildren about vegetables. He didn't preach.

Twenty-five years later, North Karelia's cardiovascular mortality had fallen by 80%. The country's life expectancy went up by ten years. The work spread to the rest of Finland.

That project — *Pohjois-Karjala-projekti* — is one of the most successful public-health interventions in human history. It worked because it didn't try to change individuals one at a time. It changed the *defaults* of an entire culture, slowly, gently, with the people, not against them.

## Why this matters for Hungary

Hungary today has the cardiovascular profile that Finland had then. About a quarter of adults are obese, another third overweight, four in ten have hypertension, and we lose about six years of life expectancy compared to the EU average. The trends are getting worse, not better.

This isn't a personal-failure problem. It's a cultural-default problem. We grew up being told to clean our plates. We pour sour cream on already-rich food. We drink more alcohol per capita than almost any country on earth. The defaults are wrong, and individuals are losing decades of healthy life trying to swim against them.

So when the founder of Kalmio thought about what to build, he didn't think about another macro-tracking app. He thought about a tool that could change defaults — quietly, gently, without preaching, in the language of the people it serves. A tool that doesn't ask you to clear your fridge of *every* familiar food, but does help you reach for the lighter csirkepaprikás more often, and the főzelék on Wednesday, and the salmon for Friday because it's nearly the weekend.

## The name

The Finnish word for *triangle* is **kolmio**. Kalmio is named in tribute — both to the North Karelia project and to the three pillars that the eventual product is built around: diet, movement, mental health and sleep. Each pillar a corner; each one supporting the others.

For now, the focus is the first corner. Diet is what we're building this year. The other two come once we've earned the right to add them.

## Why now, why us

The founder is from Hungary. His mother is a testbed user. His employer happens to be Tesco — a position with both opportunity and responsibility, which we take seriously. The team is small. The technology is real. The mission is honestly held.

We don't think a meal-planning app will save the country on its own. We do think the right tool, used by enough Hungarian households over enough years, can shift defaults in the same direction North Karelia's interventions shifted Finnish ones. Quiet, persistent, cultural change.

If that resonates, you're in the right place.
