## The forgotten ingredient

Open your fridge. Look at the produce drawer. There's something in there you bought eight days ago with good intentions, and it's now half-soft and slightly suspicious. You will probably throw it out tomorrow.

That moment — the small private grief of food going to waste — happens in millions of households every week. Hungarian households throw out an estimated 65 kg of food per person per year. That's roughly 50 000 Ft per person, per year, in food that went straight from a supermarket bag to a bin.

Most meal planners ignore this entirely. They generate a clean weekly plan as if you had an empty fridge. You don't.

## The ritual we're building

Before generating each new plan, Kalmio walks you through your fridge in a 2–3 minute "grooming" session.

Each item shows its expiry status — fresh, use soon, expired — and you tap one of three actions per item: keep, throw out, or *I have less than this*. Items past their date default to throw-out. Items close to their date get pinned for the upcoming plan, so the planner pulls them in first instead of letting them die alone.

It's quick, dignified, and ends with a single button: **Plan my week.**

## Why a ritual instead of an inventory

Inventory apps don't work. We've all tried. The cold-start problem (logging everything you already own) plus the ongoing-drift problem (you ate half a cucumber, now what?) makes them shelfware within days.

The grooming ritual is the opposite. It's not an inventory; it's a moment. Two or three minutes once a shopping cycle. The system already knows what you bought (we built the shopping list); the ritual is just confirming what's still there and what to forget about.

That low-friction loop is what makes waste reduction work in real life — instead of a guilty leaderboard, it's a small ceremony before the next plan.

## Why this becomes a differentiator

Most meal planners stop at "here's what to cook." Kalmio also tells you what to throw out — and learns from it. Two months in, the system has a better picture of what you actually use, what tends to go bad in your house, what package sizes leave you with leftovers. The next plan is a little better than the last. The plan after that is better still.

Reducing waste isn't a polish feature for us. It's central. We're a Hungarian product, built for households that feel the price of every kilogram thrown away.
