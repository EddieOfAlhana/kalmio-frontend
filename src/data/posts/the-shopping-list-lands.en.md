## The cart with three quiet duplicates

You build a plan for the week. Saturday morning you're standing in the Tesco at the corner, list in one hand, phone in the other. Item one: tej. You already have tej. Item two: paprika. There's a half-bag of paprika in the cupboard you forgot about. Item three: csirkemellfilé, 800 g — but the package on the shelf is 600 g, and the planner just told you 1.2 kg. Are you supposed to buy two? One and a half? You don't know.

You buy two, because cooking is later and you have to leave the shop.

That cart — with its three quiet duplicates and its rounding errors — is what we've been trying to fix.

## What just shipped

The shopping list arrives this week. It does three small things that ordinary lists do not.

First: **it subtracts the fridge.** Whatever the grooming ritual confirmed is still there gets pulled out of the totals before the list is rendered. If you have 250 g of joghurt left and the plan needs 300 g, the list says 50 g, not 300.

Second: **it groups by aisle the way a Hungarian shop is actually laid out** — `Zöldség–gyümölcs`, `Tejtermék`, `Hús`, `Pékáru`, `Konzerv`, `Egyéb`. Six lanes. The order roughly matches how you walk through a Tesco or a Spar. You're not jumping around the floor anymore.

Third: **it rounds to package sizes.** If the plan needs 1.2 kg of chicken and the local pack is 600 g, the list shows `2 csomag (1.2 kg)` and tells you the overshoot is 0 g. If the plan needs 850 g and the pack is 600 g, it shows `2 csomag (1.2 kg) — 350 g többlet`, and pins the surplus to a "leftover" tag the next plan can use up.

## Why this matters more than it looks

There's a depressing piece of research from the diet-and-nutrition app industry: roughly 70 percent of users drop a meal planner inside two weeks. The most common reason isn't the recipes. It's the friction at the shop. You did the planning work, you turned up with a list, and the list lied to you. Tomorrow you skip Kalmio and just buy what's familiar.

A shopping list that subtracts the fridge and rounds to real package sizes is not a glamorous feature. It is, however, the difference between a plan that survives the first Saturday and a plan that doesn't.

## What's missing (on purpose)

We're not doing barcode scanning. We're not doing in-store optimization. We're not partnering with anyone yet to let you tap-to-buy from the list. Those are real features. They're not the first feature. The first feature is: when you stand in the shop on Saturday morning, the list is honest about what you need.

The Kifli / Tesco / Wolt cart integration is coming, and it will sit on top of exactly this engine. The list is the load-bearing piece. Everything else snaps on.

## A small ask

If you take this list to the shop this weekend, tell us what was wrong with it. Wrong aisle order. Wrong default package size. Forgot to subtract something obvious. The in-app feedback panel is fastest. Hungarian shops are not uniform, and our defaults will only be as good as the corrections we get back.

The plan you made on Sunday is supposed to survive Saturday morning. That's the work.
